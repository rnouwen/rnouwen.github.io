### Project description

When fact-checking (e.g. political) discourse, cases of hyperbole are often the easiest false statements to catch, since they present matters to be implausibly extreme. But they are also easily dismissed as harmless, since one may think that hyperbole is merely an exaggeration for rhetorical effect. Still, hyperboles can be - and regularly are - deceptive, as they trigger inferences that may or may not be true. Surprisingly, we have no theoretical or empirical grasp of such inferences, nor of the role they play in deceptive uses of exaggeration. We will collect data both on the interpretation of hyperboles and on what affects people's perception of the sincerity of an exaggerated statement. We develop predictive computational models that capture the difference between honest and dishonest hyperbole. We do this by adopting a completely novel perspective, namely that hyperbole is the linguistic manifestation of the general cognitive utility of exaggeration.
